# The one-dicritical `S6` local passports

## Claim boundary

Assume that a hypothetical topological-degree-six plane Keller map has exactly
one dicritical component and the surviving passport

\[
G=6T16=S_6,
\qquad
(e,d)=(2,1).
\]

This note classifies every finite local multiplicity pattern allowed by
Orevkov's defect identity.  It proves that a jump

\[
\kappa(t)=\mu_t-2>0
\]

is exactly an intrinsically singular branch, that its connected local
monodromy is `S_(2+kappa)`, and that every jump has one of the exact
torus-knot types

\[
T(2,3),\qquad T(3,4),\qquad T(4,5).
\]

The result is a classification of necessary local passports, not an
elimination of the `S6` case.  Explicit finite analytic germs realize every
unibranch row, and an explicit collision germ shows that a zero-jump double
collision need not be omitted.

## 1. Exact fiber census

The unique dicritical maps with tangential degree one to the branch
normalization

\[
B^\nu\cong\mathbb A^1.
\]

For `t in B^nu`, let `x_t` be the corresponding boundary point and define

\[
\kappa(t)=\mu_{x_t}-2\ge0.
\]

Orevkov's exact identity gives

\[
\sum_{t\in B^\nu}\kappa(t)=3. \tag{1.1}
\]

For a target value `y in B`, put

\[
r_y=\#\nu^{-1}(y),
\qquad
\kappa_y=\sum_{t\in\nu^{-1}(y)}\kappa(t).
\]

Each normalization point contributes local boundary degree
`2+kappa(t)`.  The one-dicritical finite-normalization boundary has no hidden
codimension-two points, and every affine preimage has multiplicity one because
the original Keller map is etale.  Constant multiplicity six therefore gives
the exact formula

\[
\boxed{
\#F^{-1}(y)=6-2r_y-\kappa_y.
}
\tag{1.2}
\]

In particular,

\[
2r_y+\kappa_y\le6,
\qquad
y\notin F(\mathbb A^2)
\Longleftrightarrow
2r_y+\kappa_y=6. \tag{1.3}
\]

The complete table is:

| `r_y` | jump distribution | boundary block sizes | affine fiber size |
|---:|---|---|---:|
| 1 | `0` | `2` | 4 |
| 1 | `1` | `3` | 3 |
| 1 | `2` | `4` | 2 |
| 1 | `3` | `5` | 1 |
| 2 | `0+0` | `2+2` | 2 |
| 2 | `0+1` | `2+3` | 1 |
| 2 | `0+2` | `2+4` | 0 |
| 2 | `1+1` | `3+3` | 0 |
| 3 | `0+0+0` | `2+2+2` | 0 |

No other row fits (1.1)--(1.3).  The executable function
[`one_dicritical_s6_fiber_profiles`](../scripts/six_sheet_monodromy.py)
enumerates these rows, and its regression test locks every block shape and
omission flag.

Three points are worth keeping explicit.

- A triple normalization collision is zero-jump and necessarily omitted.
- A zero-jump double collision has two affine preimages and is **not**
  omitted.
- Noninjectivity of `B^nu -> B` alone does not force an omitted value in this
  passport.

## 2. The exact local monodromy blocks

Fix `t` and put

\[
m_t=2+\kappa(t).
\]

The connected local component at `x_t` has degree `m_t`.  Its only local
discriminant is the individual target branch belonging to `t`: other target
branches through the same value are covered without ramification by this
source component and may be filled back in.

The complement of one irreducible plane branch germ is normally generated by
a meridian.  Its image in the local permutation group is a transposition,
because the generic normal index is two.  A transitive permutation group
normally generated by a transposition is the full symmetric group.  Indeed,
make a graph whose vertices are the local sheets and whose edges are the
generating conjugate transpositions.  Transitivity makes the graph connected,
and edge transpositions of a connected graph generate the full symmetric
group.  Hence

\[
\boxed{J_t=S_{2+\kappa(t)}.} \tag{2.1}
\]

At a target value with several normalization branches, the finite source over
a small ball splits into the corresponding boundary neighborhoods and the
affine etale neighborhoods.  A meridian of one branch acts trivially on every
other block, since those local maps extend unramified across that branch.
Conjugating such a meridian still acts trivially on the other blocks, so its
normal closure supplies the whole symmetric group on its own block and the
identity elsewhere.  Thus the complete local image is

\[
\boxed{
J_y\cong
\prod_{t\in\nu^{-1}(y)}S_{2+\kappa(t)},
}
\tag{2.2}
\]

with `6-2r_y-kappa_y` additional fixed sheets.

Every finite local image is therefore intransitive on all six sheets.  Global
`S6` arises only after transport and conjugation between different parts of
the branch curve.

## 3. Jumps are intrinsic singularities

If `kappa(t)=0`, Orevkov's Lemma 5.2 makes the dicritical map a nonsingular
local embedding at `t`.  Its individual target branch is smooth.

Conversely, suppose `kappa(t)>0` but that individual branch were smooth.  The
local component is branched only over that smooth germ.  Its complement has
fundamental group `Z`, so its monodromy is the cyclic group generated by one
transposition.  That action has no transitive orbit larger than two,
contradicting local degree `2+kappa(t)>2`.  Therefore

\[
\boxed{
\kappa(t)>0
\Longleftrightarrow
\text{the individual target branch at }t\text{ is singular}.
}
\tag{3.1}
\]

This separates an intrinsic unibranch singularity from a collision of several
individually smooth normalization branches.

## 4. A contracted source point is impossible

With one dicritical, Orevkov's Lemma 2.1 gives one linear boundary chain

\[
L_C-E.
\]

Suppose that the connected constant chain `L_C` were nonempty.  Its minimal
contraction would produce a nonsmooth Hirzebruch--Jung cyclic quotient germ
`(X,x_0)`.  The unique ramification prime is the endpoint curvette `D`, of
generic index two.  In its universal quasi-etale cyclic cover

\[
q:(\mathbb C^2,0)\longrightarrow(X,x_0),
\]

the reduced inverse image of `D` is one smooth axis, say `x=0`.  For
`h=\rho\circ q=(u,v)`, pullback of the different gives

\[
J_h=\varepsilon x. \tag{4.1}
\]

The cyclic deck representation is small.  An invariant linear form would
give its generator an eigenvalue one and hence a pseudoreflection, impossible
for this quasi-etale quotient.  Both `u` and `v` are invariant, so

\[
\operatorname{ord}u,\operatorname{ord}v\ge2,
\qquad
\operatorname{ord}J_h\ge2,
\]

contradicting (4.1).  Therefore

\[
\boxed{L_C=\varnothing.} \tag{4.2}
\]

The complete proof, including the simultaneous index-three parity argument
for `A6`, is in
[`one-dicritical-source-smoothness.md`](one-dicritical-source-smoothness.md).
The finite normalization and its ramification curve are consequently smooth
at every finite point.

## 5. Every jump is an exact torus knot

Let `x_t` be a jump point and put

\[
m=\mu_{x_t}=2+\kappa(t)\in\{3,4,5\}.
\]

At this smooth source point, the ramification divisor is smooth and reduced.
Its Jacobian has a simple zero, so the differential has rank one.  Choose
source and target coordinates in which the finite germ is

\[
f(x,z)=(x,g(x,z)).
\]

After subtracting `g(x,0)` from the second target coordinate, assume
`g(x,0)=0`.  Local degree `m` gives

\[
g(0,z)=cz^m+O(z^{m+1}),
\qquad c\ne0.
\]

The reduced smooth critical curve is `g_z=0`.  Since `m>=3`,
`g_zz(0,0)=0`; smoothness of `g_z=0` forces

\[
a=g_{xz}(0,0)\ne0.
\]

Solving the critical equation as `x=h(z)` gives

\[
h(z)=-\frac{mc}{a}z^{m-1}+O(z^m).
\]

Substitution in the second target coordinate gives

\[
g(h(z),z)=-(m-1)cz^m+O(z^{m+1}).
\]

The critical image therefore has valuations `(m-1,m)`.  They are coprime, so
there are no later Puiseux pairs.  The exact types are:

| `kappa` | local degree | valuations | link |
|---:|---:|---|---|
| 1 | 3 | `(2,3)` | `T(2,3)` |
| 2 | 4 | `(3,4)` | `T(3,4)` |
| 3 | 5 | `(4,5)` | `T(4,5)` |

Thus the table classifies every positive-jump branch, with no exceptional
source case.

## 6. A double-branched-cover quotient at every jump

Let `K_t` be the knot of a positive-jump branch and put `m=2+kappa(t)`.
Equation (2.1) gives a surjection

\[
\pi_1(S^3\setminus K_t)\twoheadrightarrow S_m
\]

which sends a meridian to a transposition.  The sign character is the unique
nontrivial mod-two character of a knot group.  Its kernel is the fundamental
group of the unbranched double cover of the knot exterior, and its image is
`A_m`.  Passing to the double branched cover fills the lift of the squared
meridian, which already maps to the identity.  Therefore

\[
\boxed{
\pi_1(\Sigma_2(K_t))\twoheadrightarrow A_{2+\kappa(t)}.
}
\tag{6.1}
\]

In particular:

- for `kappa=1`, the quotient is `A3=C3`;
- for `kappa=2`, the quotient is `A4`, whose abelianization is `C3`;
- for `kappa=3`, the quotient is the nonabelian simple group `A5`.

Since the first homology of a knot's double branched cover has order equal to
the knot determinant, the first two cases imply

\[
3\mid\det(K_t). \tag{6.2}
\]

This matches `det(T(2,3))=det(T(3,4))=3`.  For the jump-three knot `T(4,5)`,
the useful condition is the stronger `A5` quotient in (6.1), not an abelian
divisibility statement.

## 7. The global jump trichotomy

The positive values of `kappa(t)` sum to three.  Exactly one of the following
occurs.

### Type `3`

There is one jump-three branch.

- Its local group is `S5`.
- Its target value has one affine preimage and cannot be a normalization
  collision.
- The required normalization collision occurs at a different value and uses
  only zero-jump smooth branches.
- Its branch is exactly `T(4,5)`, and its double-branched-cover group
  surjects onto `A5`.

### Type `2+1`

There are separate jump-two and jump-one normalization points.

- Their local groups are `S4` and `S3`.
- Both branch-knot determinants are divisible by three.
- The jump-two and jump-one branches are exactly `T(3,4)` and `T(2,3)`.
- A jump-two branch may collide with one zero-jump branch; that value is
  omitted.
- A jump-one branch may collide with one zero-jump branch; that value has one
  affine preimage.
- The jump-two and jump-one branches cannot collide with one another, since
  their boundary degrees already sum to seven.

### Type `1+1+1`

There are three jump-one normalization points.

- Every local block is `S3` and every branch-knot determinant is divisible by
  three.
- All three branches are exact `T(2,3)` cusps.
- At most two jump branches can share one target value.  If two do, their
  `3+3` boundary degrees exhaust the fiber and the value is omitted.
- A jump-one branch may instead collide with one zero-jump branch, leaving one
  affine preimage.

These three trichotomy rows and the nine fiber rows in Section 1 exhaust the
one-dicritical `S6` local passports.

## 8. Hostile analytic models

For `m=3,4,5`, consider the finite polynomial germ

\[
\Phi_m(a,z)=\bigl(a,z^m+az\bigr).
\]

Its critical curve and discriminant are parametrized by

\[
a=-mt^{m-1},
\qquad
b=-(m-1)t^m.
\]

The ramification divisor is smooth and reduced, generic inertia is a
transposition, the origin has local degree `m`, and the local group is `S_m`.
Thus `Phi_3`, `Phi_4`, and `Phi_5` realize the exact smooth-source types
`T(2,3)`, `T(3,4)`, and `T(4,5)`.  Adding identity sheets realizes the three
unibranch rows inside degree six.  In particular, a smooth jump-three `S5`
block is locally consistent.

There is also an explicit warning against treating every collision as
omitted.  For `q>=1`, the polynomial parametrization

\[
\nu_q(t)=\bigl(t^2-1,t(t^2-1)^q\bigr)
\]

has the unique collision `t=1~-1` at the origin.  Its two branches are

\[
v=\pm u^q\sqrt{1+u}
\]

and have arbitrary intersection multiplicity `q`.  Taking one smooth double
cover branched over each branch and adding two identity sheets gives local
block profile

\[
S_2\times S_2
\]

on `2+2` sheets, with two fixed affine sheets.  The collision has zero jump
and is not omitted, exactly as the `r=2`, `0+0` row predicts.

These are local analytic covers, not global Keller maps.  They prove that the
surviving rows cannot be removed by multiplicity, local knot parity, or branch
contact alone.

## 9. The next exact target

The one-dicritical `S6` case is reduced to:

- one of the three jump partitions in Section 7;
- exact local symmetric blocks of degrees three, four, and five;
- every jump branch among three explicit torus-knot types;
- at least one separate normalization collision with one of the exact fiber
  rows in Section 1.

The remaining contradiction, if one exists, must couple these local pieces
through global Keller data: braid monodromy between singular values, or the
Domrina--Orevkov splice and canonical-divisor equations on the compactification
graph.
