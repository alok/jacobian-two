# Six-sheet monodromy and the one-dicritical frontier

## Claim boundary

This note records necessary conditions on a hypothetical complex plane Keller
map

\[
F=(P,Q):\mathbb A^2_{\mathbb C}\longrightarrow\mathbb A^2_{\mathbb C},
\qquad \det JF\in\mathbb C^\times,
\]

whose **topological degree** is

\[
N=[\mathbb C(x,y):\mathbb C(P,Q)]=6.
\]

The topological degree is the generic number of points in a fiber. It is not
`deg P`, `deg Q`, a ramification index, or the order of a monodromy group.

There are two levels of conclusion:

1. for an arbitrary six-sheet counterexample, affine local inverse branches
   first force its transitive monodromy group to be one of seven GAP groups;
   Orevkov's budget, boundary symmetry, purity, and local monodromy then
   eliminate all three imprimitive survivors.  A Riemann--Hurwitz refinement
   of Orevkov's exact budget then eliminates `A5` and `S5`, leaving only
   `6T15=A6` and `6T16=S6`;
2. under the additional hypothesis that the compactified map has exactly one
   dicritical component, Orevkov's Euler-characteristic identity restricts the
   local inertia to six groups, and the absence of nontrivial polynomial deck
   transformations first reduces these to four primitive groups;
   Lin--Zaidenberg topology then eliminates two local types, leaving only
   `6T15=A6` with `(e,d)=(3,1)` or `6T16=S6` with `(e,d)=(2,1)`.

These are filters, not existence results. None of the surviving abstract groups
has been realized by a Keller map, and nothing below proves or disproves
`JC(2)`. The finite-group enumeration is a repository-derived calculation; no
claim of mathematical priority is made for it.

## 1. The nonproperness curve and an affine fixed sheet

Let `B=S_F` be the nonproperness set. Jelonek's Theorem 15 says that the
nonproperness set of a dominant generically finite polynomial map
`C^n -> C^n` is either empty or a hypersurface. For a hypothetical Keller
counterexample it is nonempty, so in the plane it is an algebraic curve.
Outside `B`, the map is a proper unramified degree-six covering

\[
F:F^{-1}(\mathbb C^2\setminus B)\longrightarrow\mathbb C^2\setminus B.
\]

The following elementary observation supplies more information than the mere
existence of `B`.

### Proposition 1.1 — the omitted set is finite

The complement `C^2 - F(C^2)` is finite.

**Proof.** The constant nonzero Jacobian makes `F` an etale morphism. Etale
morphisms are open, so `F(C^2)` is Zariski open and its complement is closed.
Suppose that the complement had an irreducible curve component `V(h)`, where
`h in C[u,v]` is irreducible and nonconstant. Then

\[
h(P(x,y),Q(x,y))
\]

would have no zero in `C^2`, hence would be a unit of `C[x,y]`. But `P,Q` are
algebraically independent: `dP wedge dQ != 0` makes the substitution map
`C[u,v] -> C[x,y]` injective. Thus `h(P,Q)` cannot be constant. This is a
contradiction. A zero-dimensional closed subset of `C^2` is finite. `square`

### Corollary 1.2 — fixed-sheet inertia

Let `B_i` be any irreducible component of `B`, let `b` be a generic smooth
point of `B_i`, and let `gamma_i` be a small positive meridian around `B_i`.
The monodromy permutation `sigma_i in S_6` of `gamma_i` has a fixed point.

Indeed, Proposition 1.1 lets us choose `b` in the image of `F`. Every affine
preimage of `b` is an etale point. The analytic inverse-function theorem gives a
single-valued local inverse branch near that preimage, and continuation around
`gamma_i` returns that sheet to itself.

This argument concerns an affine sheet. It does not say that inertia is trivial:
other sheets can run to infinity and be permuted nontrivially.

## 2. Normal generation and the seven general candidates

Set

\[
V=\mathbb C^2\setminus B,
\qquad
U=F^{-1}(V).
\]

The variety `U` is a nonempty Zariski-open subset of the irreducible variety
`C^2`, hence is connected. Therefore the monodromy group `G <= S_6` of
`U -> V` is transitive.

Meridians of the irreducible components of `B` normally generate `pi_1(V)`.
One direct proof is to contract a loop in the simply connected ambient space
`C^2`, perturb the contracting disk to meet the smooth locus of `B`
transversally, and remove small disks around the intersection points. The
boundary relation expresses the original loop as a product of conjugates of
component meridians.

Consequently, `G` is normally generated by fixed-point permutations. Equivalently,
if `G_fix` denotes the subgroup generated by all elements of `G` that fix at
least one of the six sheets, then

\[
G_{\mathrm{fix}}=G. \tag{2.1}
\]

There are exactly sixteen conjugacy classes of transitive subgroups of `S_6`.
The dependency-free certificate in
[`scripts/six_sheet_monodromy.py`](../scripts/six_sheet_monodromy.py)
enumerates each group from exact GAP generators and computes `G_fix` by closure.
The relevant output is:

| GAP id | abstract group | `|G|` | `|G_fix|` | survives (2.1)? |
|---|---|---:|---:|---|
| `6T1` | `C6` | 6 | 1 | no |
| `6T2` | `S3` | 6 | 1 | no |
| `6T3` | `D12` | 12 | 6 | no |
| `6T4` | `A4` | 12 | 4 | no |
| `6T5` | `C3 x S3` | 18 | 9 | no |
| `6T6` | `C2 x A4` | 24 | 8 | no |
| `6T7` | `S4` | 24 | 24 | yes |
| `6T8` | `S4` | 24 | 24 | yes |
| `6T9` | `S3 x S3` | 36 | 18 | no |
| `6T10` | `(C3 x C3) : C4` | 36 | 18 | no |
| `6T11` | `C2 x S4` | 48 | 48 | yes |
| `6T12` | `A5` | 60 | 60 | yes |
| `6T13` | `(S3 x S3) : C2` | 72 | 36 | no |
| `6T14` | `S5` | 120 | 120 | yes |
| `6T15` | `A6` | 360 | 360 | yes |
| `6T16` | `S6` | 720 | 720 | yes |

Thus every hypothetical six-sheet plane Keller counterexample satisfies

\[
G\in\{6T7,6T8,6T11,6T12,6T14,6T15,6T16\}. \tag{2.2}
\]

This is an exhaustive finite-group consequence of fixed-sheet inertia. It is
only a necessary condition. In particular, `6T11` can satisfy (2.1) by using
more than one fixed-point conjugacy class; no single allowed class in the
one-dicritical analysis below normally generates `6T11`.

The two regular actions `6T1` and `6T2` are the Galois degree-six cases and are
also excluded by Campbell's Galois-case theorem. Condition (2.1) gives an
independent way to see that regular actions cannot occur here: every nonidentity
element of a regular permutation group is fixed-point-free.

## 3. Orevkov's exact defect budget

Orevkov resolves the rational extension of `F` at infinity. In his notation:

- `L_infty` consists of boundary components mapped to infinity;
- `L_FC` consists of the remaining boundary components whose images meet the
  affine target;
- `L_C` is the union of components of `L_FC` on which the resolved map is
  constant;
- `L_F=L_FC-L_C` is the union of nonconstant, affine-image components — the
  dicritical components in the present terminology.

He contracts `L_infty` to the point `infty` and each connected component of
`L_C` to a point. This produces a continuous constant-multiplicity map

\[
f^*:\widetilde X^*\longrightarrow X^*
\]

of global multiplicity `N`. For an irreducible component `l subset L_F`,
`mu_l f*` is the generic **local** topological multiplicity at a point of `l`.
Orevkov's Lemma 3.1 gives the generic local analytic form

\[
(s,t)\longmapsto (s,t^e),
\]

so `mu_l f*=e` is the normal ramification index and the cycle length of a
generic local meridian. It is not `N`.

Lemma 4.2 on PDF page 7 (journal page 593) states

\[
\sum_{l\subset L_F}
\left[
  \mu_l f^*
  +
  \sum_{x\in\pi(l)\setminus\{\infty\}}
  \left(\mu_x f^*-\mu_l f^*\right)
\right]
=N-1. \tag{3.1}
\]

Every jump in the inner sum is a nonnegative integer. Corollary 4.3 gives

\[
\sum_{l\subset L_F}\mu_l f^*\le N-1. \tag{3.2}
\]

The final remark on PDF page 10 (journal page 596) says that a Keller map cannot
have a component `l` with `mu_l f*=N-1`.

For `N=6`, equation (3.1) is the exact defect budget `5`. In particular, a
generic local ramification index `e=5` is excluded. This statement must not be
misread as an exclusion of global topological degree five; the latter is the
separate theorem of Żołądek.

## 4. The conditional one-dicritical filter

Assume now that the resolved map has exactly one dicritical component `E`.
Then its image `B=F(E)` is irreducible and is the entire nonproperness curve.
The converse is not asserted: several dicritical components can dominate the
same irreducible curve.

Borisov records that every Keller counterexample has a ramified dicritical
component. Under the one-dicritical hypothesis, the generic normal ramification
index `e` of `E` is therefore greater than one. Orevkov's budget and final remark
then give

\[
e\in\{2,3,4\},
\qquad
\sum_x(\mu_x-e)=5-e. \tag{4.1}
\]

Let

\[
d=\deg(E\longrightarrow B)
\]

be the generic tangential degree. At a generic smooth point of `B`, there are
`d` points of `E` above it, each contributing one `e`-cycle. All remaining
sheets are fixed. Corollary 1.2 says at least one fixed affine sheet is present,
so `ed <= 5`. Hence the possible generic inertia types are exactly:

| `e` | `d` | meridian cycle type | total jump defect `5-e` | possible positive-jump partitions |
|---:|---:|---|---:|---|
| 2 | 1 | `(2,1,1,1,1)` | 3 | `3`, `2+1`, `1+1+1` |
| 2 | 2 | `(2,2,1,1)` | 3 | `3`, `2+1`, `1+1+1` |
| 3 | 1 | `(3,1,1,1)` | 2 | `2`, `1+1` |
| 4 | 1 | `(4,1,1)` | 1 | `1` |

The displayed jump defect is Orevkov's raw sum `5-e`.  Section 7 sharpens it:
Riemann--Hurwitz forces `e(d-1)` of those units, leaving only `5-ed` units of
genuine excess.  Thus the `(2,2)` row has raw jump defect three but residual
excess only one.

Because `B` is irreducible, its meridians form one conjugacy class. Thus a
single permutation of the displayed cycle type must normally generate the
transitive group `G`. Enumerating conjugacy classes separately gives:

| `(e,d)` | normally generated transitive groups |
|---|---|
| `(2,1)` | `6T16 = S6` |
| `(2,2)` | `6T7 = S4`, `6T12 = A5`, `6T15 = A6` |
| `(3,1)` | `6T15 = A6` |
| `(4,1)` | `6T8 = S4`, `6T14 = S5`, `6T16 = S6` |

Therefore a one-dicritical six-sheet counterexample must have

\[
G\in\{6T7,6T8,6T12,6T14,6T15,6T16\}. \tag{4.2}
\]

This is the Orevkov-plus-inertia filter. Section 5 uses the finite
normalization to remove `6T7` and `6T8`, then uses global branch-curve topology
to eliminate the `(2,2)` and `(4,1)` types from the four primitive groups.

There are two bookkeeping traps in this computation.

- `6T7` has two distinct conjugacy classes with the same ambient `S6` cycle
  type `(2,2,1,1)`. One has normal closure of order `4`, but the other has
  normal closure all of `6T7`, of order `24`. Deduplicating by cycle type before
  checking conjugacy classes incorrectly removes `6T7`.
- `6T11` has order `48`. Every one of its classes with a cycle type in the first
  table has normal closure of order at most `24`, so no single such class
  normally generates it. It survives the general multi-component test (2.2)
  because several fixed-point classes together generate `6T11`.

The regression tests in
[`tests/test_six_sheet_monodromy.py`](../tests/test_six_sheet_monodromy.py)
lock both distinctions.

## 5. One dicritical component: deck rigidity and branch topology

There is a further exact consequence of having only one dicritical component.
It uses the finite normalization, rather than merely the covering over the
complement of `B`.

Let

\[
K=\mathbb C(P,Q),\qquad L=\mathbb C(x,y),
\]

and let `W_0` be the normalization of the affine target `A^2` in `L`. It is a
normal affine surface finite over the target. Zariski's Main Theorem factors the
quasi-finite etale map `F` as an open immersion

\[
j:U=\mathbb A^2_{\rm source}\hookrightarrow W_0
\]

followed by the finite normalization map `rho:W_0 -> A^2_target`.

### Lemma 5.1 — boundary primes are dicritical components

The codimension-one components of `W_0-U` are in bijection with the
dicritical components on a resolved compactification.

To see this, compactify the finite normalization and resolve its birational
source, obtaining the Stein factorization

\[
Z\mathop{\longrightarrow}^{\tau}\overline W
\mathop{\longrightarrow}^{\rho}\overline Y.
\]

If `D` is a prime divisor of `W_0-U`, finiteness of `rho` makes `rho(D)` a
curve in the affine target. The unique strict transform of `D` on `Z` is
therefore type `3`. Conversely, a type-`3` curve `E` cannot be contracted by
`tau`: if `tau(E)` were a point, the finite map `rho` would also send `E` to a
point. Thus `tau(E)` is a prime divisor of `W_0-U`. Birationality between
normal surfaces gives uniqueness at the generic points.

Over the affine target this finite normalization is the algebraic model of the
analytic Stein factor used in Orevkov's contraction. In particular, at a
finite point his `mu_x f*` is the local degree of the corresponding connected
component of `rho`, and the generic `mu_l f*=e` is the ramification index of
the associated boundary prime. This is the bridge between the defect identity
and the local monodromy arguments below.

Under the one-dicritical hypothesis, write `D` for this unique boundary prime.
The map is etale on `U`, while the ramified-dicritical theorem cited by Borisov
makes `D` ramified. Every element of `Aut_K(L)` preserves the integral closure
`W_0` and its ramification locus, so it fixes `D` setwise.

### Lemma 5.2 — there are no hidden codimension-two boundary points

In fact

\[
W_0-U=D. \tag{5.1}
\]

This follows directly from the theorem on complements of affine opens. The
surface `W_0` is separated and Noetherian, while `U` is a dense affine open.
[Stacks Project, Lemma 31.17.5](https://stacks.math.columbia.edu/tag/0BCQ)
says that every irreducible component of `W_0-U` has codimension one. Lemma
5.1 and the one-dicritical hypothesis give only one such component, namely
`D`; therefore the whole complement has underlying set `D`. In particular,
there are no extra isolated boundary components.

### Proposition 5.3 — the function-field extension has no deck symmetry

\[
\operatorname{Aut}_K(L)=1. \tag{5.2}
\]

An element `alpha in Aut_K(L)` fixes `D`, hence by (5.1) preserves
`U=W_0-D`. It therefore restricts to a polynomial automorphism of the affine
plane satisfying

\[
F\circ\alpha=F.
\]

The group `Aut_K(L)` is finite because `L/K` is finite separable, so `alpha`
has finite order. Ali's finite-order theorem says that every finite-order
polynomial automorphism of `C^2` is polynomially conjugate to a linear one;
in particular, `alpha` has a fixed point `p`. Since `F` is a local analytic
isomorphism at `p`, the equality `F alpha=F` makes `alpha` the identity near
`p`, and hence the identity polynomially. This proves (5.2).

If `M/K` is the normal closure, `G=Gal(M/K)`, and `H=Gal(M/L)`, then

\[
\operatorname{Aut}_K(L)\cong N_G(H)/H.
\]

The exact certificate computes this quotient. It has order two for `6T7` and
`6T8`, and is trivial for `6T12`, `6T14`, `6T15`, and `6T16`. Combining
(4.2) with (5.2) leaves precisely

\[
G\in\{6T12=A5,\;6T14=S5,\;6T15=A6,\;6T16=S6\}. \tag{5.3}
\]

All four actions are primitive. More precisely, the surviving possibilities by
local type are:

| `(e,d)` | groups after the trivial-deck filter |
|---|---|
| `(2,1)` | `6T16 = S6` |
| `(2,2)` | `6T12 = A5`, `6T15 = A6` |
| `(3,1)` | `6T15 = A6` |
| `(4,1)` | `6T14 = S5`, `6T16 = S6` |

The following local observation will also be used in the unrestricted case.

### Lemma 5.4 — a four-sheet double transposition cannot be the only branch

Let a connected local component of a finite normal surface cover have local
degree four. Suppose that its reduced discriminant is one irreducible plane
curve germ and that a meridian acts on its four sheets as `(12)(34)`. This is
impossible.

Indeed, the complement of an irreducible plane curve germ is normally
generated by a meridian, by the same disk-transversality argument as in
Section 2 applied inside a Milnor ball. Hence its local monodromy group
`J <= S4` is transitive and normally generated by `(12)(34)`. All
`S4`-conjugates of a double transposition lie in the Klein four group `V4`, so
`J <= V4`. But `V4` is abelian, and the normal closure in `J` of the one
chosen double transposition is then only its order-two cyclic subgroup. It
cannot equal a transitive degree-four group.

The hypothesis that this is the **entire** local discriminant matters. For
example, knowing only that one branch is an irreducible cusp would not exclude
a second discriminant branch whose meridian supplies another generator.

### Proposition 5.5 — the `(e,d)=(2,2)` critical passport is forced

Under the one-dicritical hypothesis, suppose `e=2` and `d=2`. Then the unique
critical point of the induced degree-two map to the branch normalization has
local multiplicity five. It contributes the whole jump defect `5-2=3`, and
there are no other jumps.

To prove this, write

\[
r:E^\circ\cong\mathbb A^1\longrightarrow B^\nu
\]

for the map to the normalization of the branch curve. It is finite of degree
two. Its extension to projective completions shows that the completion of
`B^nu` is rational and that its affine part has exactly one missing point;
thus `B^nu` is isomorphic to `A1`, and after choosing coordinates `r` is a
quadratic polynomial. Let `c` be its unique critical point, let `x` be the
corresponding point of the finite normalization, and put `y=rho(x)`. The
tangential local degree two and normal index two give

\[
\mu_x\ge 2\cdot2=4.
\]

Orevkov's budget gives `mu_x <= 5`. The branch germ at `y` is unibranch. In
fact, if a second point `q' != r(c)` of `B^nu` also mapped to `y`, then `q'`
would have two regular preimages under the quadratic `r`. Those two points
would contribute at least `2+2`, in addition to the contribution at least four
at `x`, contradicting global multiplicity six.

There is only one boundary prime, and it is ramified. Purity of the branch
locus therefore says that the whole reduced discriminant of the finite
normalization is `B`, not merely that `B` is one of its components. If
`mu_x=4`, the connected local component at `x` would have degree four. At a
nearby smooth point of `B`, the two points of `E` which coalesce at `c` each
supply an `e=2` cycle, so its meridian on this component is `(12)(34)`.
Lemma 5.4 gives a contradiction. Hence `mu_x=5`; its jump `mu_x-e=3`
saturates (3.1).

The germ cannot be smooth: the complement of a smooth germ has cyclic
fundamental group, whose image generated by `(2,2,1)` is not transitive on the
five local sheets. At every other point the jump is zero, so Orevkov's Lemma
5.2 gives a nonsingular branch; the multiplicity-six bound again excludes two
normalization branches meeting there. Thus `y` is the unique finite
singularity of `B`, and it is unibranch.

This sharpens both one-dicritical `(2,2)` entries, `6T12=A5` and `6T15=A6`.
At the purely local group-theoretic level a five-sheet orbit normally generated
by the restricted cycle type `(2,2,1)` is possible. Proposition 5.7 uses the
global topology of the resulting contractible branch curve to eliminate this
local type after all.

### Proposition 5.6 — the `(e,d)=(4,1)` branch has one singular point

Under the one-dicritical hypothesis, suppose `e=4` and `d=1`. Equation (3.1)
forces a unique jump point `x` with `mu_x=5`. The degree-one map
`E^circ -> B^nu` is an isomorphism. Moreover, `E^circ -> B` is injective: two
distinct points in one fiber would contribute at least `4+4>6`. Thus every
germ of `B` is unibranch.

The unique boundary prime is ramified, so purity again makes `B` the entire
reduced discriminant. The germ at `y=rho(x)` cannot be smooth. Otherwise its
local complement would have cyclic fundamental group, while its meridian has
cycle type `(4,1)` on the connected degree-five local component; the generated
cyclic group has orbits `4+1`, not one orbit of size five. At every other point
the jump vanishes, and Orevkov's Lemma 5.2 makes the branch smooth there.
Consequently `y` is the unique finite singularity of `B`, and it is
unibranch.

At the purely local level this passport is possible: `F20=C5:C4` occurs for
`6T14=S5`, while `F20` and `S5` occur for `6T16=S6`. The next proposition
uses global branch-curve topology to exclude the type.

### Proposition 5.7 — the branch normalization cannot be injective

For any one-dicritical six-sheet Keller counterexample, the normalization map

\[
\nu:B^\nu\longrightarrow B
\]

is not injective.

First, `B^nu` is isomorphic to `A1` for every tangential degree `d`. Indeed,
the finite map

\[
E^\circ\cong\mathbb A^1\longrightarrow B^\nu
\]

extends to a nonconstant map between projective completions. The target
completion is rational, and every point missing from `B^nu` must lift to the
single missing source point at infinity. Since `B^nu` is affine, exactly one
point is missing.

Suppose now that `nu` were injective. A finite bijective morphism of complex
curves is a homeomorphism, so `B` would be topologically contractible.
The Lin--Zaidenberg theorem, in the form of Gurjar and Miyanishi's Theorem 2,
says that after a polynomial change of target coordinates

\[
B=\{X^m=Y^n\},\qquad \gcd(m,n)=1. \tag{5.4}
\]

For positive real `r`, the weighted dilation

\[
\delta_r(X,Y)=(r^nX,r^mY)
\]

preserves both `B` and its complement. Every positive dilation orbit in the
complement meets a fixed small three-sphere exactly once: the squared norm

\[
r^{2n}|X|^2+r^{2m}|Y|^2
\]

is strictly increasing from zero to infinity. If `K` is the link cut out by
`B` on a sphere `S^3_epsilon`, orbit scaling therefore gives a homeomorphism

\[
(S^3_\varepsilon\setminus K)\times(0,\infty)
   \;\cong\; \mathbb C^2\setminus B.
\]

Under the same homeomorphism, the complement in the open ball of radius
`epsilon` is exactly the subproduct with second coordinate in `(0,1)`.
Its inclusion into the global complement is consequently a homotopy
equivalence. In particular, the local-to-global map on fundamental groups is
an isomorphism.

On the other hand, consider the connected degree-six finite normalization
cover over `A2-B`. The unique boundary prime is ramified and purity makes `B`
its entire branch locus. Choose any point `x` of that boundary prime over the
origin. Orevkov's budget gives `mu_x <= 5`, while the local degrees at all
points of the central fiber sum to six. Hence that fiber contains at least one
point other than `x`. Finiteness makes the normalization map proper, so
disjoint neighborhoods of the finitely many central-fiber points separate the
inverse image of a sufficiently small target ball into at least two disjoint
open-and-closed pieces. The induced cover of the punctured local complement is
therefore disconnected, and its monodromy action on the six sheets is
intransitive. The fundamental-group isomorphism would identify this image with
the global monodromy group, which is transitive. This contradiction proves the
proposition.

### Corollary 5.8 — only two one-dicritical passports remain

The types `(e,d)=(2,2)` and `(4,1)` are impossible. For `(2,2)`, Proposition
5.5 shows that every germ of `B` is unibranch, so its normalization is
injective. For `(4,1)`, two distinct normalization points in one fiber would
contribute at least `4+4>6`, so the normalization is again injective. Both
contradict Proposition 5.7.

Consequently every one-dicritical six-sheet counterexample must have exactly
one of the following two passports:

| `(e,d)` | monodromy group | forced global branch geometry |
|---|---|---|
| `(2,1)` | `6T16=S6` | `A1 -> B` is noninjective |
| `(3,1)` | `6T15=A6` | `A1 -> B` is noninjective |

In the `(3,1)` case, any normalization collision consists of exactly two
points of local multiplicity three. Orevkov's Lemma 5.2 makes both local
branches smooth, their contributions exhaust all six sheets, and the collision
value is omitted by the original affine map.

Like the finite-group filter, Propositions 5.3–5.7 and Corollary 5.8 are
repository-derived consequences for which no priority claim is made. Their
one-dicritical hypothesis is essential.

## 6. All imprimitive candidates eliminated

The boundary argument also has consequences without the one-dicritical
hypothesis.

### Lemma 6.1 — nontrivial deck symmetry needs an unramified dicritical

If `Aut_K(L)` is nontrivial, at least one dicritical component has generic
ramification index `e=1`.

Indeed, Lemma 5.1 and the complement-of-affine-open theorem identify the whole
boundary `W_0-U` with the union of its dicritical prime divisors. If every such
divisor were ramified, this boundary would be the divisorial ramification locus
and hence would be invariant under every deck automorphism. Its complement `U`
would then also be invariant, and the Ali/local-inverse argument in Proposition
5.3 would make every deck automorphism trivial.

Each of `6T7`, `6T8`, and `6T11` has deck group `C2`, so Lemma 6.1 applies to
all three.

### Proposition 6.2 — `6T11` is impossible

For `6T11 = C2 x S4`, the fixed-point conjugacy classes relevant to normal
generation have the following exact profile:

| label | cycle type | class size | normal-closure order | minimum Orevkov cost |
|---|---|---:|---:|---:|
| `A` | `(2,1,1,1,1)` | 3 | 8 | 2 |
| `B` | `(2,2,1,1)` | 3 | 4 | 2 |
| `C` | `(2,2,1,1)` | 6 | 24 | 2 |
| `D` | `(4,1,1)` | 6 | 24 | 4 |

The minimal sets of these classes whose normal closures generate all of
`6T11` are `A+C`, `A+D`, and `C+D`. Lemma 6.1 contributes an additional
unramified dicritical of cost `1`. The pairs involving `D` therefore cost at
least `1+2+4=7`, exceeding the degree-six defect budget `5`. Only `A+C` can
remain.

Equality in the budget then forces exactly three dicritical components with

\[
(e_1,e_2,e_3)=(1,2,2)
\]

and no jumps anywhere. The class-`C` double transposition must be supplied by
one `e=2` component of tangential degree `d=2`. If it came from two
`e=2,d=1` components, those two components would already consume the entire
ramified budget and no distinct component could supply class `A`.

Let `E^circ` be the affine part of the `e=2,d=2` component. Orevkov's Lemma
2.1 gives `E^circ` isomorphic to `A1`. Since all jumps vanish, Lemma 5.2 says
that

\[
g=F|_{E^\circ}:\mathbb A^1\longrightarrow B
\]

is a nonsingular local embedding at every point. The map is finite onto its
image: if `g(t)=(p(t),q(t))` and `p` is nonconstant, then `C[t]` is finite over
`C[p(t)]`, and similarly if `q` is the nonconstant coordinate. Factoring
through the normalization gives a finite degree-two map

\[
r:\mathbb A^1\longrightarrow B^\nu.
\]

After projective completion, the target normalization is `P1`; moreover its
affine part has only one missing point, because every missing target point must
lift to the single missing source point at infinity. Hence `B^nu` isomorphic to
`A1`, and `r` is a degree-two polynomial. Its derivative has a finite zero in
characteristic zero. At that point the differential
`dg = dnu composed with dr` vanishes, contradicting the local-embedding
conclusion of Orevkov's Lemma 5.2. Thus `6T11` cannot occur.

This argument includes points created by contracting `L_C`: Lemma 5.2 is
formulated on Orevkov's contracted space and explicitly concludes that the
composed branch map is a nonsingular embedding there.

### Proposition 6.3 — `6T8` is impossible

For `6T8 = S4`, the only fixed-point conjugacy class whose normal closure is
all of `6T8` has cycle type `(4,1,1)`. It requires a dicritical with
`e=4,d=1`. Lemma 6.1 supplies an `e=1` dicritical. These two components already
saturate Orevkov's budget:

\[
4+1=5.
\]

There are no other dicriticals and no jumps. Orevkov's Lemma 5.2 makes the
restriction from the affine part of the `e=4` component a local embedding
everywhere. It is globally injective as well: if two distinct points had the
same image, their local multiplicities would sum to at least `4+4>6`, contrary
to constant global multiplicity. A polynomial map `A1 -> A2` is finite onto
its image, so this injective immersion is a closed embedding. Its image `B` is
a smooth embedded affine line.

By the Abhyankar--Moh theorem, a polynomial automorphism of the target carries
`B` to a coordinate line. The `e=4` component is the sole ramification divisor
of `W_0 -> A2`; the other boundary component has `e=1`.
[Purity of the branch locus](https://stacks.math.columbia.edu/tag/0BMB) over
the smooth target rules out isolated additional branching, so the finite
normalization is etale over `A2-B`. But

\[
\pi_1(\mathbb A^2\setminus B)\cong\mathbb Z.
\]

Its connected degree-six cover has cyclic monodromy, whereas the generic
inertia `(4,1,1)` is not even transitive. This contradicts `G=6T8`.

### Proposition 6.4 — `6T7` is impossible

Identify `6T7=S4` with its action on the six two-element subsets of a
four-element set. Its only fixed-point conjugacy classes have ambient cycle
type `(2,2,1,1)`. The size-three class has normal closure `V4`, while the
size-six class consists of the vertex transpositions and normally generates
all of `S4`. Since branch meridians normally generate the global monodromy,
some branch meridian must lie in the size-six class.

There are exactly two ways for dicriticals of normal index two to supply its
two transpositions.

First suppose that two distinct `(e,d)=(2,1)` dicriticals dominate the same
branch curve `B`. They must dominate the same curve: if their images were
different, a generic meridian of either image would be a single transposition,
which does not occur in `6T7`. Lemma 6.1 also supplies an `e=1` dicritical, so

\[
2+2+1=5
\]

saturates Orevkov's budget. There are no other dicriticals and no jumps. Each
degree-one map from a ramified dicritical to `B^nu` is therefore an isomorphism
`A1 -> B^nu`, and Orevkov's Lemma 5.2 makes the normalization map immersive at
every point. It is also injective: two normalization branches at one target
point would receive a contribution of at least `2+2` from each of the two
dicriticals, exceeding global multiplicity six. Thus `B` is a smooth embedded
affine line. The two ramified divisors are the entire divisorial ramification
locus, both map to `B`, and purity makes `B` the whole branch locus of the
finite normalization. As in Proposition 6.3, its connected degree-six cover
over `A2-B` would have cyclic monodromy, a contradiction.

It remains to realize the large double transposition by one `(e,d)=(2,2)`
dicritical `E`. Its map

\[
r:E^\circ\cong A1\longrightarrow B^\nu\cong A1
\]

is quadratic. At its unique critical point `c`, the normal index and
tangential local degree give `mu_x >= 4`. The deck-forced `e=1` dicritical
leaves only two units of jump defect, so equality is forced:

\[
\mu_x=4,
\qquad
(e_1,e_2)=(1,2),
\qquad
\mu_x-e_2=2. \tag{6.1}
\]

In particular there are no further dicriticals or jumps. The branch germ at
`y=rho(x)` is unibranch by the same multiplicity argument as in Proposition
5.5: another normalization point over `y` would have two regular preimages
under `r`, contributing at least four in addition to `mu_x=4`.

Now `E` is the unique ramified prime of the finite normalization. The source
is normal, the target is smooth, and the affine source is etale. By purity of
the branch locus, every non-etale point lies on `E`; hence the **entire**
reduced discriminant is the one irreducible curve `B`. Take a sufficiently
small ball about `y` and the connected component of its preimage containing
`x`. It has degree `mu_x=4`. The two nearby points of `E` which coalesce at
`c` supply both `e=2` cycles, so a meridian acts on these four local sheets as
`(12)(34)`. This contradicts Lemma 5.4.

The exact certificate independently records the same last obstruction inside
the global six-sheet action. For a size-six double transposition `sigma`, the
subgroups `J <= 6T7` which contain `sigma` and are normally generated by its
`J`-conjugacy class have only the following sheet-orbit sizes:

| `|J|` | orbit sizes | number of such subgroups |
|---:|---|---:|
| 2 | `2,2,1,1` | 1 |
| 6 | `3,3` | 2 |
| 24 | `6` | 1 |

None has a four-sheet orbit. This completes the elimination of `6T7`.

Combining Propositions 6.2–6.4 with the seven-group filter (2.2) gives the
intermediate primitive restriction

\[
G\in\{6T12=A5,\;6T14=S5,\;6T15=A6,\;6T16=S6\}. \tag{6.2}
\]

## 7. Tangential Riemann--Hurwitz leaves only `A6` and `S6`

For every dicritical component `E`, let `e_E` be its generic normal index and
let `d_E` be the degree of its finite polynomial map

\[
E^\circ\cong\mathbb A^1\longrightarrow B_E^\nu\cong\mathbb A^1.
\]

If `a_c` is the tangential local degree at `c`, local multiplicity gives
`mu_(pi(c)) >= e_E a_c`, while finite Riemann--Hurwitz gives
`sum_c(a_c-1)=d_E-1`.  Substitution in Orevkov's exact identity produces

\[
\boxed{\sum_E(e_Ed_E+\delta_E)=5,\qquad\delta_E\ge0.} \tag{7.1}
\]

For a generic branch meridian, `sum e_E d_E` over the ramified dicriticals
dominating that branch is exactly the number of moved sheets.  Hence the
support sizes of the normally generating branch classes have total at most
five.  Exact enumeration leaves the following primitive profiles before the
last topology filter:

| group | normally generating ramified branch classes of total cost at most five |
|---|---|
| `A5` | one double-transposition class, cost 4 |
| `S5` | one 4-cycle class, cost 4 |
| `A6` | one 3-cycle, cost 3; or one double transposition, cost 4 |
| `S6` | one transposition; two transpositions; a transposition plus a 3-cycle; one 4-cycle; or one same-branch `(3)(2)` class |

If there is one ramified branch curve and its meridian moves at least four
sheets, a normalization collision would consume at least eight sheets.
Its normalization is therefore injective.  The branch is then a contractible
curve with normalization `A1`; Lin--Zaidenberg identifies its global
complement with its local weighted-homogeneous complement.  Orevkov's budget
also makes every boundary local degree at most five, so the local finite cover
is disconnected, whereas the global cover is connected.  This contradiction
eliminates the sole-branch profiles which move four or five sheets.

Consequently the unrestricted conclusion sharpens from (6.2) to

\[
\boxed{G\in\{6T15=A6,\;6T16=S6\}.} \tag{7.2}
\]

The exact surviving ramified profiles are:

- `A6`: one `(e,d)=(3,1)` branch, with residual budget two;
- `S6`: one transposition branch; two distinct transposition branches; or
  distinct transposition and 3-cycle branches.

The complete proof, including the local/global topology lemma and executable
profile table, is in
[`refined-six-sheet-budget.md`](refined-six-sheet-budget.md).

## 8. Geometry still missing from the group filter

The remaining work is geometry at infinity, not more abstract Galois theory.
Borisov's compactification gives a birational map `Z -> W` followed by a finite
map `rho:W -> Y`, and the Keller adjunction formula

\[
\overline K_W
=
\rho^*\overline K_Y
+
\sum_{\operatorname{type}(E_i)=3} e_i E_i.
\tag{8.1}
\]

Here type `3` means dicritical and `e_i` is its normal ramification index.
Borisov further proves or records the following constraints:

- target augmented-canonical labels are nonpositive;
- target determinant labels are nonnegative (Corollary 8, page 24);
- boundary curves generate the Mori cone (Theorem 9, page 24);
- once the image of the original line at infinity has become a curve, images of
  dicritical components meet only augmented-canonical-label-zero boundary
  curves (Theorem 11 and its consequence, pages 25–26);
- maps above trivalent target vertices are rational Belyi maps, so their branch
  data are constrained by Riemann–Hurwitz (pages 23 and 26).

Domrina and Orevkov proved the analogous one-dicritical stratum impossible in
topological degree four (Theorem 0.2, page 1). Their proof enumerates the finite
splice-diagram possibilities and combines determinant identities with the
canonical-divisor form of the Keller condition. Domrina then eliminated the
remaining two-dicritical degree-four cases. Żołądek's Theorem 6.12 extends the
full low-sheet result through topological degree five. No cited source performs
the corresponding degree-six enumeration.

The sharp next target exposed by this note is therefore:

> **One-dicritical degree-six problem.** Prove that no topological-degree-six
> Keller map has exactly one dicritical component.

Corollary 5.8 leaves only two local types, two groups, and five coarse positive
jump partitions: `(e,d)=(3,1)` with `6T15=A6`, or `(2,1)` with `6T16=S6`.
The first is a particularly narrow next stratum. Its normalization `A1 -> B`
must identify points; every collision consists of exactly two smooth local
branches, exhausts all six sheets above an omitted target value, and consumes
none of the remaining jump defect two.  The local analysis in
[`a6-one-dicritical-local.md`](a6-one-dicritical-local.md) rules out splitting
those two units as `1+1`: there is one unique local-degree-five point.  If its
source is smooth, its target branch is a `(2,5)` cusp.  Eliminating this `A6`
stratum would leave only the `S6` passport, but this repository does not yet
claim that elimination.

The complementary local analysis in
[`s6-one-dicritical-local.md`](s6-one-dicritical-local.md) gives all nine
finite fiber rows for the `S6,(e,d)=(2,1)` passport.  A jump
`kappa=mu-2` has local group `S_(2+kappa)`, and every jump away from the
at-most-one contracted source singularity is exactly `T(2,3)`, `T(3,4)`, or
`T(4,5)`.  Explicit local germs realize those rows, so this is again a sharp
restriction rather than an elimination.

For the unrestricted six-sheet problem, configurations with several dicritical
components must still be handled.  Section 7 reduces their monodromy to `A6`
or `S6`, but a combinatorial framework is still only necessary data: Borisov
explicitly warns that solving the associated large systems of equations would
be needed to produce an actual Keller map. His displayed frameworks have
smallest topological degree `16`, not `6` (Question 6, page 27).

## 9. Reproduction

Run the exact certificate and focused tests with:

```bash
uv run python scripts/six_sheet_monodromy.py
uv run pytest tests/test_six_sheet_monodromy.py
# Optional independent GAP cross-check, when SageMath is installed:
sage tools/check_six_sheet_gap.sage
```

The runtime calculation does not call GAP or Sage. The checked-in generators
are the degree-six transitive-group generators exported from GAP 4.14.0; the
module rebuilds every finite group, conjugacy class, normal closure, stabilizer,
normalizer, and block system using a small pure-Python permutation engine.
